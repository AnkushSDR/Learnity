Is Matter Around Us Pure?

1. Charactertistics of Matter

What is Matter?

Matter is anything that occupies space and has mass. It can exist in different forms, and based on its composition, it is classified into two main types:

  • Pure Substances:
  • A pure substance is made up of only one kind of particle (atom or molecule).
  • It has a fixed composition and uniform properties.
  • Elements are the simplest form of matter.
  • Compounds are substances formed when two or more elements chemically combine in fixed proportions (e.g., water, cardon dioxide).
  • Mixtures:
  • Mixtures consist of two or more substances physically combined, not chemically.
  • They do not have a fixed composition or definite properties.

    • Charactertistics of Particles of Matter

    1. The particles of matter are very, very small.
    2. The particles of matter have spaces between them.
    3. The particles of matter are constantly moving.
    4. The particles of matter attract each other.

    How does matter move?

    It is known to us that particles of matter are constantly moving, according to the characteristics of matter. How do they actually move? They move in random irregular ways in the atmosphere. The rapid and random ways of movement of tiny particles suspended in air shows that air is made up of particles and they move constantly. The zig-zag or random movement of the small suspended particles in a liquid (or a gas) is called Brownian motion.

    Summary

  • Matter are of two types Pure, Unpure, Elements, Compounds and Mixtures.
  • Pure substances have one kind of particle with fixed composition and uniform properties.
  • Compounds are formed from two or more substances that are chemically combined in fixed proportions.
  • Mixtures consist of two or more substances physically combined, not chemically that are not of fixed composition or definite properties.
  • Particles move in random, irregular ways, especially in the atmosphere.
  • The zig-zag or random movement of small suspended particles in a liquid or gas is called Brownian motion.

    2. Forms of Matter

    Meaning of Rigid and Fluid

    Before we give the definitions of solids, liquids and gases, we should know the meaning of two new terms: rigid and fluid. Rigid means 'unbending' or 'inflexible'. A stone is rigid because it is unbending or inflexible. Fluid means 'a material which can flow easily' and requires a vessel (or container) to keep it. Water is a fluid because if we pour water on the top of a table, it flows over the surface of the table. Moreover, water requires a vessel to keep it. Milk is a fluid and chlorine gas is also a fluid.

    Solids

    A solid is a rigid form of matter. Due to their rigidity, solids have a tendency to maintain their shape when subjected to outside force. Thus, the main identifying characteristic of solids is their rigidity. Solids do not require a container to keep them. A brick and a log of wood are two common solids.

    Liquids

    A liquid is a fluid form of matter which occupies the lower part of the container in which it is kept. A liquid does not fill the whole container. Being fluids, liquids require a container to keep them. A liquid has a well-defined surface, so a liquid can be kept in an open container. The liquid will not escape from the open container by itself. Water and milk are two common liquids around us.

    Gases

    A gas is a fluid form of matter which fills the whole container in which it is kept. Being fluids, gases also require a container to keep them. A gas does not have a free surface, so a gas has to be kept in a closed container. A gas will escape if kept in an open container. This is why gases are usually kept in air-tight gas cylinders. For example, cooking gas (LPG) is kept in air-tight metal cylinders. From this discussion we conclude that both, liquids and gases are known as fluids. Please note that the characteristic of liquids and gases of 'flowing' easily is called 'fluidity'. Due to fluidity, liquids and gases change their shapes readily when subjected to outside force.

    Summary

  • Rigid materials, such as stones, maintain their shape and are inflexible, while fluids, like water and milk, can flow and require containers to hold them.
  • Solids are characterized by their rigidity and do not require a container to maintain their shape; examples include bricks and logs.
  • Liquids occupy the lower part of their container and have a well-defined surface, ensuring they do not escape an open container by themselves.
  • Gases fill their entire container, lack a defined surface, and must be kept in closed containers to prevent escape.
  • Both liquids and gases are classified as fluids due to their ability to flow easily and change shape when external forces are applied.
  • The property of easily flowing is known as 'fluidity,' which is a key characteristic of liquids and gases.
  • Understanding the differences between these states of matter is essential for various scientific and practical applications.

    3. Classification and properties of Solids, Liquids and Gases

    Classification of the forms of Matter

    On the basis of physical states, all the matter can be classified into three groups: Solids, Liquids and Gases. For example:

    (i) Sugar, sand, iron, wood, rocks, minerals and ice are solids.
    (ii) Water, milk, oil, kerosene, petrol and alcohol are liquids, and
    (iii) Air, oxygen, hydrogen, carbon dioxide and steam are gases.

    Solids, liquids and gases are called the three states of matter. In other words, matter exists in three physical states: solid, liquid and gas. The solids, liquids and gaseous forms of matter have different properties. These properties can be used to distinguish between the three states of matter: solid, liqudis.

    Properties of Solids

    Ice, wood, coal, stone, iron, brick, etc., are some of the common solids around us. The solids have the following characteristic properties:

    1. Solids have a fixed shape and a fixed volume.
    2. Solids cannot be compressed much.
    3. Solids have high densities. They are heavy.
    4. Solids do not fill their container completely.

    Properties of Liquids

    Water, milk, fruit juice, ink, groundnut oil, kerosene and petrol, etc., are some of the common liquids. The liquids have the following characteristic properties:

    1. Liquids have a fixed volume but they have no fixed shape. Liquids take the shape of the vessel in which they are placed.
    2. Like solids, liquids cannot be compressed much.
    3. Liquids have moderate to high densities. They are usually less dense than solids.
    4. Liquids do not fill their container completely.
    5. Liquids generally flow easily.

    Properties of Gases

    Air, oxygen, hydrogen, carbon dioxide, nitrogen and steam, etc., are some of the common gases. The gases have the following characteristic properties:

    1. Gases have neither a fixed shape nor a fixed volume. Gases acquire the shape and volume of the vessel in which they are kept.
    2. Gases can be compressed easily (into a small volume).
    3. Gases have very low densities. They are very, very light. A gas is much lighter than the same volume of a solid or a liquid.
    4. Gases fill their container completely.
    5. Gases flow easily.

    Kinetic Theory of Matter

    According to the kinetic theory of matter, the particles (and possess kinetic energy). Some forces of attraction also exist between the particles of matter. These are called interparticle forces. The forces of attraction tend to hold the particles together and control their movements. The interparticle forces of attraction are the maximum when the particles are close together. As the distances (or spaces) between the particles of matter increase, the forces of attraction between them decrease. The movement of particles (or kinetic energy of particles) can be changed by heating the matter (or cooling it). Higher the temperature, greater is the movement of particles. We can now say that the following properties of particles determine the physical state of matter. That is, the following properties of particles decide whether a given substance will exist as a solid, a liquid or a gas:

    1. The spaces (or distances) between the particles. The spaces (or distances) between the particles are the minimum in solids, a little more in liquids, and the maximum in gases.
    2. The force of attraction between particles. The forces of attraction between the particles (or interparticle forces) are the strongest in solids, less strong in liquids and negligible in gases.
    3. The amount of movement of particles (or kinetic energy of particles). The movement of particles (or kinetic energy of particles) is the minimum in solids, more in liquids and the maximum in gases.

    Summary

  • Matter can be classified into three states: solids, liquids, and gases.
  • Solids, such as sugar and steel, maintain a fixed shape and volume, are incompressible, and have high density.
  • Liquids, including water and oil, have a fixed volume but assume the shape of their container, are also incompressible, and flow easily.
  • Gases like oxygen and carbon dioxide do not have a fixed shape or volume, can be compressed into smaller volumes, and fully occupy their containers.
  • Solids do not fill their container completely, while liquids and gases do.
  • Liquids typically have densities that are lower than solids but variable, while gases have very low densities.
  • All three states of matter have unique properties that make them distinguishable from one another.
